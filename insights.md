# Key Insights — Bank Customer Churn Analysis

Analysis of 10,000 bank customer records to identify churn drivers and 
provide retention recommendations.

## 1. Geography is a major churn driver
Germany has a churn rate of 32.44% — nearly double that of Spain (16.67%) 
and France (16.17%) — despite all three having comparable customer bases.
**Recommendation:** Investigate region-specific service issues, fee structures, 
or competitor activity in Germany, and launch a targeted retention campaign 
for German customers.

## 2. Holding more products is linked to dramatically higher churn
Customers with 1-2 products churn at 27.71% and 7.60% respectively, but 
those with 3-4 products churn at 82.71% and 100%. This is counterintuitive — 
typically more products signals loyalty — and suggests multi-product 
customers may be overwhelmed, dissatisfied with cross-sold products, or 
experiencing service complexity.
**Recommendation:** Review the onboarding and service experience for 
customers with 3+ products; investigate whether these are forced/bundled 
products rather than genuinely chosen ones.

## 3. Customers aged 50-59 are the highest-risk segment
This age group churns at 56.04%, compared to just 7.56% for 18-29 year-olds 
and 10.88% for 30-39 year-olds — a sharp jump beginning around age 40-49 
(30.83%).
**Recommendation:** Design retention offers (loyalty pricing, dedicated 
relationship managers) specifically for the 40+ segment, who may be 
reassessing their banking relationships around this life stage.

## 4. Inactive members churn at nearly 2x the rate of active members
Inactive members churn at 26.87% vs. 14.27% for active members.
**Recommendation:** Build an engagement program (app nudges, periodic 
check-ins, personalized offers) targeting customers who show declining 
activity, before they become full churn risks.

## 5. Female customers churn more than male customers
Female customers churn at 25.07% vs. 16.47% for male customers.
**Recommendation:** Explore whether product fit, marketing, or service 
experience differs by gender, and tailor retention outreach accordingly.

## Ruled Out
- **Tenure** showed minimal correlation with churn (~19-23% across all tenure years).
- **Credit card ownership** showed negligible difference (20.20% vs 20.81%).

## Overall Recommendation
The bank should prioritize retention efforts on customers in Germany, aged 
40+, holding 3 or more products, and showing reduced account activity — 
this combination represents the highest concentration of churn risk. A 
targeted, proactive outreach program for this segment is likely to yield 
the best return on retention spend.
